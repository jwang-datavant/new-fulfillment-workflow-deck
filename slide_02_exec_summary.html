Slide 2 — Executive Summary
Executive Summary
Fulfilling a patient records request today requires one person to do multiple jobs — sequentially. There's a better way.
01
How it works today
A single records request can span five separate systems — EHRs like Cerner and Epic, an imaging system, archival storage, and a billing system. One operations staff member handles all of them: logging in and out of each, physically walking to the radiology department, calling out for archival boxes. Every task is done one at a time, in sequence.
02
Why it's a problem
Sequential fulfillment means total time = the sum of every task. One delay cascades into everything else. Constantly switching between systems degrades quality and creates errors. Worse, because we don't track tasks individually, we have no data on where time is actually going — which means we can't build a roadmap to automation.
03
Our hypothesis
We believe we can split each request into parallel task units and route each to the right processor at the same time — digital retrieval via Connections, onsite tasks to a specialist, remote tasks to an offshore team. Total time drops to the slowest single task, quality improves through specialization, and we gain the task-level data needed to systematically automate.
The Ask
Design + Ops resources to prototype and validate this hypothesis in Q3 — before committing to a build. Eng scoping commitment (Connections + ROI Zone) for Q4 if the hypothesis is confirmed.
